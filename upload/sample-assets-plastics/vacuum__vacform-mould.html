Vacuum Forming

In school workshops, vacuum forming moulds are made from a variety of materials such as wood, medium density fibreboard (MDF), plaster of paris and clay. MDF is probably the most suitable for "one-off" vacuum formings or short production runs. Having no grain, MDF is relatively quick and easy to shape compared with solid wood and is also stable whilst being heated in the vacuum forming machine.

In industrial situations, where higher volumes of production are required, it is necessary to manufacture moulds from harder wearing materials such as aluminium or cast epoxy resin. The photo above shows an aluminium mould positioned on the platen of a vacuum forming machine. This pattern is used to produce the base part of a shop display board.

It is necessary to have a 5-10° taper or draft angle on the sides of the mould to enable the forming to be separated easily from the mould. This can be seen clearly in the finished moulding which has sides that taper in towards the top.

It is important to avoid undercuts as these will also prevent the forming from releasing. Moulds which contain cavities should have vent holes (see below) drilled in positions where there is any possibility of areas of the mould being sealed by the hot thermoplastic sheet as the air is being evacuated.

Moulds should ideally have radiused corners and the depth of the draw in straight sided cavities should generally be kept below a width/depth ratio of 2:1 to avoid thinning of the thermoplastic sheet whilst vacuum forming.
